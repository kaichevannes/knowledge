## Bad Habits
1. **Don't use analogies** (comparing something you did to something they did).
  Often this ends up arguing about the analogy rather than them hearing what you
  have to say.
2. **Don't ad hominem your partner** -- don't make identity-based criticisms instead
  of behavioural ones e.g. if your partner doesn't do the dishes, just talk
  about the dishes, not "you always do this", "you're this kind of person". It
  becomes about a personality trait rather than the behaviour. Instead, sit down
  with them and lay out the concern, asking something like: "I'm not really sure
  what to do, can you help me with this?"
3. **Don't be defensive**. It is natural to become defensive when someone makes a
  criticism, we can deflect. It isn't about quid pro quo: you do it too. By
  responding that way we are rejecting the criticism. Acknowledge what they say:
  "You're right. I didn't do the dishes."
4. **Don't stonewall**. This is being physically present in a conversation but
  mentally checked out, it is a flight/freeze reaction. When we are stonewalling
  we need to take a physical break that is at least 20 minutes long. Say to
  your partner something like: "Hey, I'm not really able to hear what you're
  saying right now. I understand it's really important, can I have a bit of
  time to collect myself and then I will be able to pay attention to what you're
  saying." Not scrolling, giving your physiology a chance to calm down.
5. **Don't propose solutions**: this often invites an argument: "I don't want
  you to hang out with this person anymore because of X, Y and Z". It is much
  more effective to share our concern. We want to solve the problem together
  as partners: "I'm concerned with _. Can we figure out what to do about this?
  My instinct is to tell you not to hang with them anymore but I don't want to
  be controlling."

## Good Habits
1. **Positive greetings**: acknowledging them in some way: touching them in
  some way, giving them a quick kiss, giving a hug, saying hi. We start to
  become roommates when we don't interact in this way.
2. **Positive partings**: saying goodbye, "I can't wait to see you later", the
  content doesn't matter as long as it is signalled in some positive way: a
  small physical touch, a peck on the cheek, a quick hug. Some ritual for
  joining and parting. The Gottman institute recommends a 6 second kiss on
  parting.
3. **Express appreciation or admiration on a daily basis**: as least one or two
  times per day, putting some thought into your kindness. Things like a meal
  they prepared on one day, the way that they look on another day, the way that
  they cleaned up after you on a third day. Letting them know that they are
  valued by us.
4. **Express affection on a daily basis**: physical touch, playful, engaging in
  some kind of affectionate way.

## Tips
1. **Develop your own language**: there are distinct patterns in every
  relationship like spiralling, "I feel like hibernating", etc. You should
  come up with terms for the patterns that come up in your relationship.
2. **Quantifying preferences**: rate things from 1-10, e.g. when deciding what
  to eat one person might be a 8/10 for Thai and the other might be a 5/10 for
  pizza. In that case you can agree to eat Thai.
3. **Want tokens**: when we want something, we can make logical justifications
  for why this thing "happens" to be the objectively good for us. Separate
  things that you should do from things that you want to do. Parents should
  hang out with their childrens friends parents, that is separate from a
  sociable partner wanting to do it for themselves, even if they will enjoy it
  more than a less sociable partner. A want token is something that I just
  "want". It isn't something you should do, it's just something indulgent.
  Mixing the two causes problems.
